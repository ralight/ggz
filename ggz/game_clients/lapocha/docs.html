Dealing the Cards

La Pocha is a Spanish card game for four. The card set used is non­standard: the 7s, 8s, and 9s are removed in order to create a deck of size forty.

Dealing proceeds in order from a randomly chosen person. The responsibility for dealing the cards circulates among the players in counterclockwise fashion. The number of cards dealt depends on which hand is being played. It is easier to simply list the hand sizes, rather than explain them; here is the list:

After the cards are dealt, the remainder of the deck is cut to determine the trump suit for the hand. During the middle four hands of the game, where all forty cards are dealt, there will be no cards left to cut. When this occurs, the dealer is presented with the opportunity to choose the trump suit after looking at his cards.

Bidding

The person to the left of the dealer starts the bidding stage, declaring the number of tricks he will take. Bidding continues around the circle, until it reaches the dealer. There is a restriction placed on the dealer's bid: the number of tricks that he bids may not make the total tricks bid equal to the total number of tricks in the hand.

Example A: Assume that one card is dealt to each player, and that the first three bids are 1, 0, and 0 (total of one). The dealer, in this case, is forbidden to bid that he will not take a trick, even if he is certain he could not do so, as this would cause the total bid to equal one.
Example B: Assume that eight cards are dealt to each player, and that the first three bids are 3, 2, and 0 (total of five). The dealer, in this case, is forbidden to bid three tricks, as this would cause the total bid to equal eight.

No player can bid more tricks than the number of cards in his hand. This may seem obvious but it could be to his advantage to do so if he were trying to reach a final score of zero - thus there is an explicit rule against doing so.

Game Play

Game play proceeds much as any other "meet your bid" type of game. First the player to the dealer's left plays an initial card. Play then continues clockwise to the left. Players must follow the suit of the first card played in each trick. As is typical, the highest card in the led suit takes the trick unless a trump card was played - in which case the highest trump card wins the trick.

If a player does not have the suit which was led, then he must play a higher trump card than any trump card currently on the table. If he cannot play a higher trump, then he may choose not to play trump.

Example A: Assume the following game-play:
  • Spades is trump
  • Player 1 plays a Heart
  • Player 2 follows with a Heart
  • Player 3 has no Hearts and trumps with King of Spades
If player 4 has no Hearts, but his highest Spade is a Jack, then he may choose to play the Jack or a card of another (non-trump) suit. If he has a Heart, he must play it.
Example B: Assume the following game-play:
  • Clubs is trump
  • Player 1 plays a Diamond
  • Player 2 has no Diamonds or trump, and plays a Heart
  • Player 3 has no Diamonds, and plays the Two of Clubs
  • Player 4 bid one trick and has already made it
If player 4 has no Diamonds, he must play trump as long as he has any trump card higher than the 2, even though he has already made his bid and it is not in his best interests to trump.

Winning the Game

Scoring is handled as follows:

  • Each player which bids correctly (precisely the proper number of tricks) gets ten points, plus five points for each trick they bid.
  • Each player which bids incorrectly (too many or too few tricks taken) loses five points per trick they bid.

Winning conditions:

  • The player with the greatest number points wins.
  • If a player should achieve exactly zero points when all play is complete, they are deemed to be the winner.
